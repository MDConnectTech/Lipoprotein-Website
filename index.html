Lipoprotein(a) [Lp(a)] — Patient Information

Lp(a) is a cholesterol-carrying particle that is mostly determined by genetics. It is not always included in routine cholesterol testing, so many people don’t know their level.

At a glance

A quick overview before you dive into the details.

What it is

Lipoprotein(a) [Lp(a)]

Lp(a) is a type of lipoprotein (a “carrier” in the blood). Like LDL, it contains ApoB, but Lp(a) also includes an additional protein called Apo(a). Higher levels can contribute to plaque buildup in arteries.

Why it matters

Risk can be “silent”

People with high Lp(a) can have increased risk for heart attack, stroke, peripheral artery disease (PAD), and aortic stenosis. Many people feel well until a serious event occurs.

What you can do

Use it for a plan

Lp(a) is often genetic and may not change much. The goal is to reduce overall cardiovascular risk with the right targets and treatments for you.

Should I ask for an Lp(a) test?

Many people only need Lp(a) checked once. It can be especially helpful if heart disease runs in your family.

Good reasons to ask

  • Early heart disease in the family (heart attack or stroke at a younger age)
  • A parent, sibling, or child has high Lp(a)
  • Familial hypercholesterolemia (FH)
  • Heart disease despite “normal” routine cholesterol results

How to get tested

1
Ask for Lp(a)

It’s a simple blood test. Ask your clinician to add “Lp(a)” to your labs — it’s not always included in routine cholesterol testing.

2
If elevated

If your level is high, your clinician may recommend testing close family members as well.

What do Lp(a) results mean?

Lp(a) can be reported in mg/dL or nmol/L. Your clinician interprets it in context with your overall risk.

Interpreting results

Units and thresholds

A commonly used “elevated” threshold in many patient resources is > 50 mg/dL (or > 125 nmol/L). Your clinician may use a different threshold depending on your lab and clinical situation.

If you have questions, ask your care team what your specific number means for you.

Is Lp(a) genetic?

Yes. Lp(a) is largely inherited and often stays relatively stable over a person’s lifetime.

Having high Lp(a) does not mean a heart problem is inevitable — it means your care team may recommend a more personalized prevention plan.

Next steps if your Lp(a) is high

Even if Lp(a) itself doesn’t change much, your overall risk can change a lot with the right plan.

Focus on overall risk reduction
  • Heart-healthy eating pattern
  • Regular physical activity
  • Healthy weight maintenance
  • Avoid tobacco
  • Sleep, stress management, and blood pressure control

These habits support overall cardiovascular health even if they don’t directly lower Lp(a).

Medications & therapies

What your clinician may discuss

Your clinician may recommend intensive management of other risk factors (especially LDL cholesterol). New therapies specifically targeting Lp(a) are under investigation.

Cost & insurance

Checking coverage

Coverage varies by plan. If you’re checking benefits, you can ask your insurer about Lp(a) testing using CPT code 83695.

FAQs

Tap a question to expand.

Can I have “normal cholesterol” but high Lp(a)?
Yes. LDL (“bad cholesterol”) and Lp(a) are different. Some people have a normal LDL but still have high Lp(a). Lp(a) often requires a separate test.
If Lp(a) is genetic, can lifestyle help?
Healthy lifestyle habits are still very important. They may not directly lower Lp(a), but they can lower your overall heart risk (blood pressure, weight, blood sugar, and LDL).
How often should I check Lp(a)?
Because Lp(a) is usually stable over time, many people only need it checked once, unless your clinician recommends repeat testing for a specific reason.
What should I ask my clinician?
Ask whether Lp(a) testing is appropriate, how your result changes your risk assessment, whether relatives should consider testing, and what LDL and blood pressure targets are right for you.
