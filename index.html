Lipoprotein(a) / Lp(a)

Lipoprotein(a) [Lp(a)] — Patient Information

Lp(a) is a cholesterol-related particle you inherit from your family. You can have healthy habits and still have a high level — that’s why knowing your number can help you and your clinician plan.

Mostly genetic Not on many routine panels Helps guide prevention

Provided by Central Florida Heart Care. Educational information only (not medical advice).

Key takeaways

  • It’s common to have normal LDL but still have high Lp(a).
  • High Lp(a) can raise the risk of heart and valve disease.
  • If it runs in families, relatives may benefit from a one-time test.

Should I ask for an Lp(a) test?

Many people only need Lp(a) checked once. It can be especially helpful if heart disease runs in your family.

Good reasons to ask

  • Early heart disease in the family (heart attack or stroke at a younger age)
  • A parent, sibling, or child has high Lp(a)
  • Familial hypercholesterolemia (FH)
  • Heart disease despite “normal” routine cholesterol results

How to get tested

1
Ask for Lp(a)

It’s a simple blood test. Ask your clinician to add “Lp(a)” to your labs — it’s not always included in routine cholesterol testing.

2
If elevated

If your level is high, your clinician may recommend testing close family members as well.

FAQs

Tap a question to expand.

Can I have “normal cholesterol” but high Lp(a)?
Yes. Lp(a) is separate from LDL and often requires a separate test. You can have normal LDL and still have high Lp(a).
If Lp(a) is genetic, can lifestyle help?
Healthy habits still matter a lot for overall heart risk. Even if Lp(a) doesn’t change much, things like blood pressure, LDL, weight, sleep, and activity can.
How often should I check Lp(a)?
Many people only need it checked once, unless your clinician recommends repeating it for a specific reason.
What should I ask my clinician?
Ask what your Lp(a) result means for your personal risk, whether relatives should be tested, and what prevention targets make sense for you.
