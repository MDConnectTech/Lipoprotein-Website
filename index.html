Lipoprotein(a) / Lp(a)

Concerned about hidden heart risk?

Lp(a) is a genetic cholesterol particle that is not included in many routine cholesterol panels. Knowing your level can help guide a more personalized prevention plan.

What it is

A genetic cholesterol particle

Think of Lp(a) as a form of LDL with an extra protein attached. That extra piece can make it more likely to contribute to plaque buildup over time.

Why it matters

Risk can stay hidden

High Lp(a) can increase risk for heart attack, stroke, and aortic valve disease even when a person otherwise feels well.

Who should ask

Good reasons to test

Testing may be especially helpful when heart disease runs in the family, when a close relative has high Lp(a), or when cardiovascular disease appears despite routine cholesterol results that look acceptable.

What results mean

Think bigger than one number

Many resources use above 50 mg/dL or above 125 nmol/L as elevated, but your clinician interprets the result together with your full risk picture.

Frequently asked questions

The reference site relies heavily on expandable FAQs near the bottom. Your existing FAQ content already fits that pattern well.

Can I have normal cholesterol but still have high Lp(a)?
Yes. Lp(a) is different from standard cholesterol numbers and usually requires a separate test.
If Lp(a) is genetic, can lifestyle still help?
Lifestyle may not lower Lp(a) much directly, but it can still improve your overall cardiovascular risk in a meaningful way.
How often should I check Lp(a)?
Many people only need it checked once because it is usually stable, although a clinician may recommend repeat testing in specific cases.
What should I ask my clinician?
Ask whether Lp(a) testing is appropriate for you, what your result means, what LDL goal fits your situation, and whether family members should be tested.
