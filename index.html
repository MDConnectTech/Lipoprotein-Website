Lipoprotein(a)

Medical Information for Patients

What is Lipoprotein(a)?

Lipoprotein(a), abbreviated Lp(a), is a genetically determined lipoprotein particle similar to LDL cholesterol. Elevated levels are associated with increased risk of atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease and aortic valve stenosis.

Why It Matters

High Lp(a) levels are an independent risk factor for cardiovascular disease. Unlike LDL cholesterol, Lp(a) levels are largely inherited and are not significantly affected by diet or lifestyle.

Who Should Be Tested?

  • Individuals with premature cardiovascular disease
  • Family history of early heart disease
  • Unexplained high LDL cholesterol
  • Recurrent cardiovascular events despite standard therapy

How Is It Measured?

Lp(a) is measured with a blood test. Testing is generally recommended at least once in adulthood, particularly in individuals with cardiovascular risk factors.

Treatment Considerations

Currently, no widely approved therapies specifically lower Lp(a) significantly. Management focuses on aggressive control of other cardiovascular risk factors, including LDL cholesterol. Emerging therapies targeting Lp(a) are under clinical investigation.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can diet lower Lp(a)?
Lp(a) levels are primarily genetically determined and typically do not respond substantially to dietary modification.

How often should it be tested?
In most individuals, Lp(a) testing is needed only once unless clinically indicated otherwise.
