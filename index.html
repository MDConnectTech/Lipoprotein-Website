Lp(a) / Lipoprotein(a)

Understanding Lipoprotein(a) — what it is, why it matters, and what to do next

Lp(a) is a cholesterol-carrying particle that is mostly determined by genetics. It is not included in a routine cholesterol (lipid panel), so many people don’t know their level.

When to get tested What results mean FAQs
Key takeaways
  • Some people can have a normal LDL number and still have high Lp(a).
  • High Lp(a) increases risk of cardiovascular disease and aortic valve disease.
  • Family members may benefit from testing (cascade screening).
What it is

Lipoprotein(a) [Lp(a)]

Lp(a) is a type of lipoprotein (a “carrier” in the blood). Like LDL, it contains ApoB, but Lp(a) also includes an additional component (Apo(a)). Too much Lp(a) can contribute to artery plaque.

Why it matters

Risk can be “silent”

High Lp(a) can contribute to plaque buildup and can also increase inflammation and clotting risk, which can be associated with heart attack, stroke, PAD, and aortic stenosis.

When should I talk to my doctor about an Lp(a) test?

Common reasons to test
  • Family or personal history of premature heart disease (under 45 for men, under 55 for women) :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}
  • Known family history of high Lp(a) :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}
  • Familial hypercholesterolemia (FH) :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}
  • No symptoms but strong family history (risk can remain hidden) :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

How do I get tested?

A standard cholesterol test (lipid panel) usually does not include Lp(a). Ask your clinician about adding an Lp(a) test. :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}

Family testing (cascade screening)

If a close family member has high Lp(a), consider testing parents, siblings, and children. :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}

What do Lp(a) results mean?

One commonly used “high” threshold

Levels higher than 50 mg/dL (125 nmol/L) are considered high in this resource. :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}

High Lp(a) is associated with increased risk of heart attack, stroke, PAD, and aortic stenosis. :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}

Is Lp(a) genetic?

Yes — Lp(a) is mostly determined by genetics. :contentReference[oaicite:10]{index=10} Levels are described as being set early in childhood and generally stable over time. :contentReference[oaicite:11]{index=11}

What can I do if my Lp(a) is high?

Lifestyle changes generally do not lower Lp(a) itself, but they can lower overall cardiovascular risk. :contentReference[oaicite:12]{index=12}

Focus on overall risk reduction
  • Heart-healthy eating pattern
  • Regular physical activity
  • Healthy weight maintenance
  • Avoid tobacco
  • Limit alcohol and prioritize sleep

Myths vs facts (plain language)

“If I know my LDL, I don’t need Lp(a).”
Lp(a) is different from LDL, and some people can have normal LDL but high Lp(a). Routine cholesterol tests may not include Lp(a), so it often requires a separate test. :contentReference[oaicite:16]{index=16}
“If I feel fine, I don’t need testing.”
Many people have no symptoms until a serious event occurs (like a heart attack or stroke). Discuss testing if you have personal or family risk factors. :contentReference[oaicite:17]{index=17}
“Only adults can be tested.”
This resource notes that Lp(a) levels are set early and generally stable; it also discusses testing and heart-healthy habits for children with high Lp(a). :contentReference[oaicite:18]{index=18}

Frequently asked questions

How common is high Lp(a)?
The playbook describes Lp(a) as common globally and cites figures such as “about 1 in 5” people worldwide in its educational materials. :contentReference[oaicite:19]{index=19}
Can diet or exercise lower Lp(a)?
Diet and exercise improve overall heart health and risk, but they do not typically lower Lp(a) levels. :contentReference[oaicite:20]{index=20}
What should I ask my clinician?
Ask whether Lp(a) testing is appropriate for you (especially with family history or premature heart disease), how your result changes your overall risk plan, and whether family members should consider testing (cascade screening). :contentReference[oaicite:21]{index=21} :contentReference[oaicite:22]{index=22}
