Lipoprotein(a) / Lp(a)

Lipoprotein(a) [Lp(a)] — Patient Information

Lipoprotein(a), or Lp(a), is a particle in your blood that is mostly inherited (runs in families). It isn’t always checked on routine cholesterol tests—so many people don’t know their level.

Often genetic Not always on a lipid panel Helpful for risk planning

Key takeaways

  • Some people can have a normal LDL and still have high Lp(a).
  • High Lp(a) is associated with higher cardiovascular risk and aortic valve disease risk.
  • Because it can run in families, relatives may benefit from testing.

At a glance

A quick overview before you dive into the details.

What it is

Lipoprotein(a) [Lp(a)]

Lp(a) is a type of “cholesterol carrier” in the blood. It’s similar to LDL, but has an extra protein attached. Higher levels can contribute to plaque buildup in arteries.

Why it matters

Risk can be “silent”

Higher Lp(a) can raise the chance of heart and blood vessel problems over time. Many people feel fine—so testing helps you plan early.

What you can do

Use it for a plan

Lp(a) usually doesn’t change much. The goal is to lower your overall heart risk by optimizing LDL, blood pressure, and healthy habits.

When should I talk to my doctor about an Lp(a) test?

Testing is especially worth discussing if you have personal or family risk factors.

Common reasons to test
  • Personal or family history of premature heart disease
  • Known family history of elevated Lp(a)
  • Familial hypercholesterolemia (FH)
  • Recurrent cardiovascular events or strong family history despite “normal” routine labs
How to get tested

Ask for an Lp(a) blood test

Ask your clinician about adding an Lp(a) test. It is not always included in a standard lipid panel.

Family testing

Cascade screening

If you have high Lp(a), discuss whether parents, siblings, and children should be tested as well.

What do Lp(a) results mean?

Your result may be reported in mg/dL or nmol/L. Ask your clinician which number matters for your lab and your risk.

Interpreting results

Units and thresholds

Many resources call Lp(a) “elevated” above > 50 mg/dL (or > 125 nmol/L). Your clinician may use a different cutoff depending on your situation.

If you have questions, ask your care team what your specific number means for you.

Is Lp(a) genetic?

Yes. Lp(a) is largely inherited and often stays relatively stable over a person’s lifetime.

Having high Lp(a) does not mean a heart problem is inevitable — it means your care team may recommend a more personalized prevention plan.

Next steps if your Lp(a) is high

Even if Lp(a) itself doesn’t change much, your overall risk can change a lot with the right plan.

Focus on overall risk reduction
  • Heart-healthy eating pattern
  • Regular physical activity
  • Healthy weight maintenance
  • Avoid tobacco
  • Sleep, stress management, and blood pressure control

These habits support overall cardiovascular health even if they don’t directly lower Lp(a).

Medications & therapies

What your clinician may discuss

Your clinician may recommend intensive management of other risk factors (especially LDL cholesterol). New therapies specifically targeting Lp(a) are under investigation.

Cost & insurance

Checking coverage

Coverage varies by plan. If you’re checking benefits, you can ask your insurer about Lp(a) testing using CPT code 83695.

Frequently asked questions

Tap a question to expand.

“If I know my LDL, I don’t need Lp(a).”
LDL and Lp(a) are different. Some people can have normal LDL but high Lp(a). Lp(a) often requires a separate test.
“If I feel fine, I don’t need testing.”
Many people with elevated risk have no symptoms until a major event occurs. Testing can help clarify risk—especially with a strong family history.
How often should Lp(a) be checked?
Because Lp(a) is usually stable over time, many people only need it checked once, unless your clinician recommends repeat testing for a specific reason.
What should I ask my clinician?
Ask whether Lp(a) testing is appropriate, how your result changes your risk assessment, and whether relatives should consider testing. Also ask what LDL and blood pressure targets are right for you.
